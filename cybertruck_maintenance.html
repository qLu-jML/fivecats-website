Annual costs Kari should anticipate for the Tesla Cybertruck Premium AWD — so she can decide whether to keep it or sell it.

For comparison: A typical full-size gas pickup (F-150, Silverado) costs roughly $3,500–$5,500/year in fuel, maintenance, and repairs — but insurance is significantly cheaper ($1,500–$2,500/year). The Cybertruck saves on fuel and maintenance but costs more for insurance and tires. With distributed compute income factored in, the Cybertruck's net annual cost could drop to within range of — or even below — a comparable gas truck.

The Easy Part

Routine Maintenance

The Cybertruck requires dramatically less routine maintenance than a gas truck. No oil changes, no transmission fluid, no spark plugs, no exhaust system, no emissions testing. Regenerative braking extends brake pad life significantly. Tesla's official position is that the Cybertruck does not require annual maintenance.

Iowa-specific note: Iowa winters with road salt make brake caliper lubrication especially important. The stainless steel body won't rust, but undercarriage components benefit from regular washing during winter. Cold weather also reduces battery range by 15–30%, so winter range will be lower than the rated 325 miles.

Bottom line: expect roughly $600/year in maintenance for the first five years. Third-party estimates from CarEdge put total maintenance at roughly $1,961 over the first five years (~$392/year), while Kelley Blue Book estimates approximately $623/year. The difference depends on what each source includes — but averaging across both, ~$50/month is a reasonable budget for routine upkeep. That's a fraction of what a gas truck costs in oil changes, transmission service, and exhaust repairs alone.

The Biggest Expense

Tires — The Real Cost

Tires are the single biggest recurring maintenance expense. The truck weighs nearly 7,000 pounds and produces over 500 lb-ft of instant torque, which accelerates tire wear. Factory tire size is 285/65R20 (approximately 35 inches tall).

With normal driving (no aggressive acceleration, mostly highway), expect to replace tires every 25,000–40,000 miles. At 12,000–15,000 miles/year, that means a new set roughly every 2–3 years.

Annualized tire cost: $400–$800/year depending on tire choice and driving style. Over 100,000 miles, total tire costs could reach $4,000–$6,000.

Tip for Kari: The 20" Core Wheels on this truck look great without aero covers. Ditching the covers opens up a much larger selection of more affordable tires. Range loss without covers is minimal — about 5–10 miles.

Ways to Lower Insurance

Shop around aggressively. Quotes vary 50–80% between carriers for the same coverage.

Raise the deductible to $1,000–$1,500 to significantly reduce premiums.

Check Tesla Insurance availability in Iowa — it uses a driving Safety Score.

Bundle with homeowner's insurance for multi-policy discounts.

If the truck is paid off, weigh whether full coverage is still worth the premium cost.

Remove duplicate coverages like roadside assistance if covered elsewhere.

Fuel Costs, Reimagined

Charging & Electricity

The Cybertruck has a 123 kWh battery and uses approximately 41 kWh per 100 miles. Charging at home is dramatically cheaper than fueling a gas truck. The truck comes with the $600 Universal Home Charger and $300 Mobile Charger already included.

If She Decides to Sell

Depreciation & Resale Value

Depreciation is the largest cost of vehicle ownership but only matters when you sell. Kelley Blue Book estimates roughly $8,283/year over five years, with the steepest drop in years 1–3.

The Decision

Keep It or Sell It?

There's no wrong answer. It depends on Kari's situation, comfort level, and financial picture at the time. Here's a simple framework.

Reasons to Keep

  • Insurance is manageable (under $300/month)
  • Home charging is set up and convenient
  • Safety features are valuable for Iowa winters
  • Maintenance costs are low and predictable
  • The truck is still under warranty
  • She enjoys driving it
  • It has sentimental value
  • Distributed compute could offset costs (Digital Optimus)

Reasons to Sell

  • Insurance exceeds $400/month
  • No convenient home charging access
  • The truck feels too large for daily driving
  • She'd rather have $40K–$65K+ in cash
  • Tire costs feel burdensome
  • She'd prefer a smaller, cheaper vehicle
  • Warranty is expiring and battery risk is a concern

New Revenue Opportunity

Earn Money While Parked

In March 2026, Tesla confirmed a new program that could change the economics of ownership: AI4-equipped Teslas can now lease out their computing power while parked. The Cybertruck's onboard AI4 chip — the same hardware that runs Full Self-Driving — sits idle most of the day. Tesla is putting that dormant power to work.

The initiative, called Digital Optimus, is a joint project between Tesla and xAI. When the Cybertruck is parked (ideally plugged in), its AI4 processor can handle AI inference tasks — pattern recognition, generative AI workloads, and other compute-heavy operations — as part of a massive distributed network.

How It Works

Detail Info
Eligible Hardware AI4 (Hardware 4) — included in the Cybertruck
AI4 Performance ~100–150 TOPS (AI operations per second)
What It Processes AI inference tasks for the xAI / Digital Optimus network
When It Runs While parked — best when plugged in at home
Status Deployment underway as of March 2026

Tesla is also deploying dedicated Digital Optimus compute units at Supercharger stations, backed by roughly 7 gigawatts of available power across the network. The fleet of parked Teslas plus Supercharger hubs creates a distributed supercomputer that rivals traditional data centers.

Looking ahead, Tesla's next-gen AI5 chip (expected mid-2027) will deliver 10x more compute and 9x more memory than AI4 — meaning the earning potential of each vehicle could grow substantially over time.

What this means for Kari: The Cybertruck could eventually earn money while sitting in the driveway. Tesla hasn't announced specific compensation rates yet, but owner opt-in and payment details are expected as the program rolls out. This is a new reason to keep the truck — it may partially offset ownership costs like insurance and charging. Worth watching as details emerge.
